Corridor / Route-Graph Preprocessing

A preprocessing stage that turns the sea into a graph: waypoint nodes where ships change course and sea-lane edges between them. That GTRA-style corridor graph becomes a prior for anomaly detection and routing across the Harbormaster platform.

In one paragraph

Vessels do not move randomly; they follow established corridors. If you know the corridors, an off-corridor track, a course change in open water far from any known waypoint, or a vessel in a lane it cannot physically use becomes a strong, explainable anomaly signal. This stage builds the corridor graph from public AIS (via RDP line simplification and HDBSCAN) and from NOAA nautical charts, then enriches it with seabed depth and seasonal ice.

Provenance, stated up front. The waypoint-detection method here is grounded in Arun's real ESRI 2023 internship work ESRI and the Singh et al. GTRA paper paper. This Harbormaster stage is an original re-implementation on public data personal. The two are kept separate throughout. See the Honesty boundary page.

What this page covers

  • The real internship work it is grounded in (the shareable waypoint detection, with results).
  • The corridor graph: nodes, edges, and how it is built two ways.
  • The deviation detector and how the graph feeds anomaly scoring, the space-time prisms, and route optimization.
  • The two context layers Arun identified during the internship but could not model then: bathymetry and weather.

The real internship work ESRI

The shareable half of Arun's ESRI 2023 internship: waypoint detection in maritime trajectories. This is the documented, reproducible foundation the corridor stage rebuilds.

The method

  • Waypoint definition: a geographic location where a vessel maneuvers or changes course en route from source to destination.
  • Detection: per-track turning-point extraction, then HDBSCAN clustering of candidates, then the mean center of each cluster as the final waypoint.
  • The lead method, Ramer-Douglas-Peucker line simplification, with a real twist: instead of a fixed global tolerance, the tolerance is set per track to the standard deviation of that track's perpendicular distances from its start-end chord. An adaptive, per-track epsilon. This is a genuine contribution beyond the reference paper.
  • Compared against: change-in-bearing (atan2 threshold), CUSUM on bearing, and Mahalanobis distance over joint bearing and speed change.

Ground truth from NOAA charts (the distinctive idea)

Because there is no public dataset of maritime waypoints, ground truth was manufactured from NOAA Electronic Navigational Charts: shipping-lane polygons run through a polygon-to-centerline operation, and the bends and intersections of those centerlines are labeled as ground-truth waypoints. AIS-derived detections are then scored against them. Charts used included 1117A, 11340, 11360, 411, 25671 LE, 11328, 11330, 18740, and 530.

Across five parameter sweeps (track count, distance, neighbors, std-dev threshold, cluster size) RDP beat change-in-bearing in every case. The graph-network anomaly detector and a novel non-threshold waypoint method were written up as future work, not benchmarked.

These waypoint results are the shareable, reproducible part of the internship and the only ESRI numbers quoted in Harbormaster artifacts. The internship's anomaly-detector results were a separate, restricted deliverable and are not used here. See the Honesty boundary.

The GTRA corridor graph personal

A graph G(V, E): waypoint nodes and sea-lane edges, built from two complementary sources.

AIS tracks NOAA ENC charts | | RDP (adaptive eps) polygon-to-centerline | | turning points lane bends / intersections | | HDBSCAN + centroid charted waypoints \\ / \\ / v v =========== WAYPOINT NODES V =========== node = (centroid lon/lat, covariance, degree, source in {AIS, ENC}) | join consecutive nodes | =========== SEA-LANE EDGES E =========== edge = (node_u, node_v, geometry, visit_weight, source) degree-2 node = straight lane ; degree>2 = intersection/waypoint

Nodes V

  • AIS-derived: RDP per-vessel waypoint reduction (the adaptive per-track tolerance) then HDBSCAN cluster then mean center. The personal re-implementation of the ESRI and paper pipeline on public AIS.
  • ENC-derived: NOAA chart shipping-lane polygons to centerlines; bends and intersections become charted waypoints. The personal re-implementation of the distinctive ESRI ground-truth idea, from public NOAA ENC data.
  • Each node carries a covariance (the cluster spread, used later as a Mahalanobis gate), a degree (degree-2 means a straight lane passing through; degree greater than 2 means a real intersection or decision point), and its source.

Edges E (the sea-lanes)

  • Route segments joining consecutive nodes, formed two ways: from RDP-simplified AIS tracks (with a normalized visit-count threshold, per GTRA edge selection) and from charted lane centerlines.
  • Each edge carries geometry, a visit weight (how heavily trafficked), and its source. Edges are where the later context layers attach: depth, expected speed, and time-varying availability.
This matches the reference paper's GTRA construction (RDP for waypoint reduction, DBSCAN to cluster waypoints into nodes, visit-threshold edge selection) and extends it with the adaptive tolerance and the NOAA-chart node seeding.

Static and learned build modes

Usable cheaply and early, rich and distributed later.

Static corridor (Phase 1)

A precomputed, versioned graph built offline from the NOAA ENC charts plus a one-time MarineCadastre extract for the demo regions (Gulf of Mexico, San Diego / LA, Houston). Stored as an Iceberg table plus a compact serialized artifact in the S3 model bucket, loaded read-only by the ECS Fargate serving container at startup.

Cost: ~$0 (artifact load). No streaming dependency. Analyst-editable. Unblocks the deviation signal in the Phase 1 slice with a frozen graph.

Learned corridor (Phase 3)

The same graph rebuilt periodically by the EMR PySpark batch job over the 600 GB MarineCadastre Iceberg lake, versioned per region per run, promoted through the same boundary as the Pi-DPM model (S3 to registry to serving load).

Cost: rides the Phase 3 backfill that already exists; auto-terminating EMR. This is where the distributed-rebuild skill is demonstrated honestly as personal work.

Storage

Iceberg tables corridor_graph_nodes and corridor_graph_edges in the existing S3 lakehouse (Glue catalog, Athena-queryable, time-travel for versioning), plus the serialized artifact for fast serving load. No new always-on service, no GPU, no ALB.

The corridor deviation detector personal

A new deterministic agent in the ECS Fargate front door, next to the gap and rendezvous detectors.

What it computes

It implements the GTRA association test: the perpendicular distance of each AIS point to the nearest sea-lane edge, with a slope-match check on intermediate samples. It emits two deterministic reasons into the same fusion and HITL path the front door already produces:

ReasonMeaningESRI framing
off_corridorThe track's perpendicular distance to the nearest sea-lane exceeds d_max: positional deviation from the known lane."Anomalous Route"
unexpected_nodeA course change occurred far from any known waypoint node: a maneuver where vessels do not normally turn."Anomalous Waypoint"

Why it sits where it sits

  • The test is geometric and cheap, so it runs inline on CPU, consistent with the platform's CPU-kernel serving assumption. No new always-on cost.
  • It is a third deterministic signal alongside STAGD gap detection and TGARD rendezvous, not a replacement. The heavy Pi-DPM head remains the async learned signal.
  • Its reasons fold into the same anomaly score, reason strings, and human-review enqueue the other agents already use, so it needs no new serving surface.
Honest tie-back: this is exactly the internship's "Anomalous Route / Anomalous Waypoint" framing, which was problem-formulated but not benchmarked at ESRI. Harbormaster implements it on the public corridor graph.

How it feeds the platform

One graph, three consumers.

1. An additional anomaly signal

The CorridorDeviationDetector adds off_corridor and unexpected_node to the deterministic signals that fuse into the anomaly score. A vessel that leaves its lane, or turns where no one turns, is flagged with an explainable reason an operator can act on.

2. Priors for the space-time prisms

The space-time prism bounds where a vessel could plausibly be during an AIS gap. Today it is a kinematic ellipse (gap endpoints as foci, a speed cap). The corridor graph sharpens it: intersect the prism with the nearest sea-lane edges so the feasible region is the prism restricted to navigable corridors rather than open water. A gap that resumes off-corridor is more suspicious, which directly improves gap-anomaly precision. Edge visit statistics also give a per-edge expected-speed prior that tightens the ellipse.

3. The action space for route optimization

The Phase 5 route-optimization policy consumes the corridor graph as its action space: feasible patrol or coverage routing is restricted to sea-lane edges and waypoint nodes, under the physical feasibility constraints. This is the honest, personal re-creation of "route optimization over a maritime graph," labeled a stretch goal.

Where it lands in AWS

Graph in Iceberg + a serialized artifact in S3; the detector runs in the existing ECS Fargate front door; the Flink job optionally pre-joins each AIS event to its nearest edge id (a cheap spatial lookup against the broadcast static graph) so edge becomes a streaming feature in DynamoDB. Promotion of the learned graph rides the same model-promotion boundary. Static mode adds ~$0; learned mode rides the existing backfill.

Bathymetry: seabed depth as navigability personal

Arun's insight from the internship, now modeled: the deeper and more stable the seabed corridor, the more a deep-draft vessel can use it. Recognized then as a driver of route choice, never modeled within the timeline.

Origin in the materials ESRI

In mentor feedback, route density was explained by "Geographic Islands, Oceanic Depth": shallow seabed governs which lanes deep-draft vessels can use. It was treated as explanatory GIS context, not a trained model.

How it enters the graph

  • Data: NOAA bathymetric soundings (the same ENC S-57 soundings already read via the maritime toolbox) and GEBCO global gridded bathymetry. Both public.
  • Per-edge navigability weight: sample the minimum charted depth along each sea-lane centerline; combine with the vessel's draught (an AIS attribute) to compute an under-keel-clearance feasibility weight.
  • Per-node clearance attribute: the shallowest safe passage near each waypoint.

What it buys

  1. Prunes or down-weights edges a given vessel cannot physically use.
  2. Sharpens the physical-feasibility gate: a deep-draft vessel "transiting" a sub-draught corridor is implausible, a real anomaly.
  3. Restricts the space-time prism to navigable-depth corridors, tightening the feasible region further.

Stored as extra columns on corridor_graph_edges; static, recomputed only when charts update. Added in Phase 3.

Weather and seasonal ice: time-varying corridors personal

Arun's Arctic insight, now modeled: many routes open only in summer when the ice melts, and it is safer to navigate there then. The corridor graph becomes time-varying.

Origin in the materials ESRI

Weather appears in the internship materials only as benign noise to filter (an AIS gap "due to weather conditions") and as a future "Out of Seasonal Activity" anomaly use case. The seasonal-route-opening framing was a motivating direction, not implemented. Harbormaster makes it a model.

How it enters the graph

  • Data: NOAA marine weather forecasts and NOAA / NSIDC sea-ice extent products. Public.
  • Time-varying edges: each sea-lane edge gets a validity window and an availability weight keyed by time. The graph becomes G(V, E_t), where edge availability is a function of time.
  • An Arctic corridor edge is active only in the open-water season and inactive (or heavily penalized) under ice. Storm-impacted edges get a transient down-weight.

What it buys

  1. Suppresses false alarms: a vessel absent from an ice-closed lane is not anomalous. This is exactly the benign-noise filtering the ESRI materials asked for.
  2. Surfaces real anomalies: a vessel transiting a corridor that should be ice-closed is genuinely suspicious.

Time-varying weights are a temporal dimension on the Iceberg edge table, refreshed by a scheduled lightweight job. No GPU. Added in Phase 5.

Both context layers are contextual GIS-layer joins onto the graph, not a multi-modal neural model, consistent with how the internship itself treated bathymetry and weather: explanatory context, never a trained model.
